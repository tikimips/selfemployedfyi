# The Complete Guide to Qualifying for a Mortgage When Self-Employed

*Target keyword: "self employed mortgage" | Estimated: 3,200 words | Status: Ready to publish*

---

If you're self-employed and trying to buy a home, you've probably run into this: you make good money, you pay your taxes, and yet the bank keeps telling you that you don't qualify for the mortgage you need.

You're not imagining it. There is a genuine, systemic disconnect between how much self-employed people earn and how much conventional lenders think they earn — and it comes down to a single page of your tax return.

This guide explains exactly how lenders calculate income for self-employed borrowers, what you can do about it, and how to figure out which path makes sense for your specific numbers.

---

## The Core Problem: What Banks Actually Look At

When you apply for a conventional mortgage as a W-2 employee, lenders look at your pay stubs and W-2s. Simple.

When you're self-employed, they look at something different: **your Schedule C net income from the last two years of tax returns**, averaged together.

That number — your net income after every business deduction — is often dramatically lower than your actual revenue. And it's that lower number they use to calculate everything: your maximum loan amount, your monthly payment, your DTI ratio.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

| Gross Revenue | Business Deductions | Schedule C Net Income | What the Bank Sees |
|---|---|---|---|
| $200,000 | $120,000 | $80,000 | $80,000 |
| $150,000 | $70,000 | $80,000 | $80,000 |
| $300,000 | $200,000 | $100,000 | $100,000 |

Two freelancers earning very different amounts can look identical to a lender.

## Why This Happens: The Deduction Paradox

The financial advice you've been getting from your CPA is good advice — for taxes. Write off your home office, your equipment, your mileage, your professional development, your business meals. Reduce your taxable income. Save money.

But those deductions don't disappear when you apply for a mortgage. They follow you into the lender's underwriting process and reduce the income figure they use to determine what you can borrow.

This is the deduction paradox: **the better you are at minimizing your taxes, the worse you look on paper to a lender**.

There's no easy fix, but there is a path — you just need to understand your options clearly.

## How Conventional Lenders Calculate Self-Employed Income

For borrowers who file Schedule C (sole proprietors, single-member LLCs), lenders follow Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac guidelines:

1. Pull Schedule C net profit from the last 2 years of 1040s
2. Average the two years together (if income is declining, they typically use the lower year)
3. Divide by 24 to get your monthly qualifying income
4. Apply the DTI ratio (typically max 43%) to determine your maximum monthly mortgage payment
5. Back-calculate to find your maximum loan amount at current rates

**Example:**
- Year 1 Schedule C net: $85,000
- Year 2 Schedule C net: $95,000
- Average: $90,000
- Monthly qualifying income: $7,500
- Max monthly payment (43% DTI): $3,225
- At 7% rate, 30-year fixed: Max mortgage of approximately $484,000

That's the math. If your target home price requires a bigger mortgage, you don't qualify conventionally — regardless of how much you actually make.

## Your Options as a Self-Employed Buyer

### Option 1: Conventional Mortgage

If your Schedule C income (averaged over two years) is high enough to support the mortgage you need, conventional is your best bet. You'll get the best rates and the most lender options.

**Best for:** Self-employed borrowers who have strong net income after deductions, or whose deductions have been modest relative to revenue.

**What you need:**
- 2 years of tax returns showing consistent or growing Schedule C income
- Debt-to-income ratio under 43%
- Credit score 620+ (680+ for best rates)
- Down payment of 3-20%

### Option 2: Non-QM Bank Statement Loan

Non-Qualified Mortgages (Non-QM) are loans that don't meet Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac guidelines — which means they're not constrained by the same income documentation rules.

**Bank statement loans** are the most common Non-QM option for self-employed borrowers. Instead of looking at your tax returns, lenders look at 12-24 months of bank deposits and use a percentage of that as your qualifying income.

**How it typically works:**
- Lender takes 12 or 24 months of business bank statements
- Applies an expense factor (commonly 50% for business accounts, sometimes higher)
- The result is your qualifying income

So if you deposit an average of $15,000/month into your business account, a lender using 50% expense factor would credit you with $7,500/month in qualifying income — regardless of what your Schedule C says.

**Best for:** Self-employed borrowers with high gross revenue but low Schedule C net income due to significant write-offs.

**Tradeoffs:**
- Higher interest rate: typically 0.5-2% above conventional rates
- Larger down payment often required (10-20%)
- Fewer lender options
- Slightly higher fees

**Is the higher rate worth it?**

It depends on your specific numbers. Sometimes yes — getting into the house you actually need at a slightly higher rate beats waiting years to restructure your business deductions. Use our calculator to model both scenarios side by side.

### Option 3: Reduce Your Deductions

This is the option CPAs hate but mortgage brokers sometimes suggest. The math usually doesn't work out.

If you reduce your business deductions, your Schedule C net income increases — and your qualifying income goes up. But so does your tax bill.

**Example:**
- You need $16,500 more in qualifying income to hit your mortgage target
- Reducing deductions by $16,500 increases your taxes by approximately $5,700 (at 35% effective rate)
- You pay $5,700 extra in taxes to qualify for a mortgage that costs you more per month than if you'd just used a Non-QM loan

Run the numbers. In most cases, Non-QM wins on pure math. In some cases — especially if your deductions are already modest — reducing them slightly may make sense.

### Option 4: Add a Co-Borrower

If you have a spouse, partner, or co-buyer with W-2 income, their income can be combined with yours for qualifying purposes.

This is often the cleanest solution if it's available to you. Their W-2 income is straightforward to document, dramatically increases your combined qualifying income, and allows you to use a conventional loan.

### Option 5: Wait for a Better Tax Picture

If you're planning ahead, you have time to structure your next 1-2 years of taxes with mortgage qualification in mind. This doesn't mean giving up legitimate deductions — it means being strategic about timing.

Talk to a CPA who understands both tax strategy and mortgage qualification. They're not the same thing, and finding someone who speaks both languages is valuable.

## Working with a Mortgage Broker

If you're self-employed and serious about buying, work with a broker — not a bank directly. Brokers have access to multiple lenders including Non-QM specialists, and they're much better equipped to navigate the complexity of self-employed income documentation.

When you interview brokers, ask:
- "Do you have experience with self-employed borrowers?"
- "Do you have access to Non-QM or bank statement loan products?"
- "What income figure would you use to qualify me?"

A broker who can't clearly answer the second question is not the right broker for you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long do I need to be self-employed to get a mortgage?**

Most conventional lenders require 2 years of self-employment history, documented with 2 years of tax returns. Some lenders will work with 1 year if you were previously employed in the same field.

**Q: Can I get a mortgage if I'm newly self-employed?**

It's harder, but not impossible. Non-QM lenders are sometimes more flexible on this. You'll generally need to show strong bank statement history even without 2 years of returns.

**Q: Does my business structure matter?**

Yes. Schedule C (sole prop/single-member LLC) is the most common but often the most impacted because all income and deductions flow through to your personal return. S-corp owners have additional considerations — lenders may also look at your W-2 from the S-corp plus your share of business income.

**Q: What if one year was much better than the other?**

If your income is increasing year-over-year, lenders typically average the two years. If it's decreasing, many lenders will use the lower year — a significant penalty. This is another reason timing your application matters.

**Q: What credit score do I need?**

For conventional: 620 minimum, 740+ for best rates. For Non-QM: typically 620-640 minimum, with rates improving at higher scores.

## The Bottom Line

Self-employed mortgages are more complex but absolutely achievable. The key is understanding exactly where you stand before you start shopping — so you're not blindsided by a denial or a pre-approval number that doesn't match your expectations.

Start with the calculator. Know your numbers. Then talk to a broker who specializes in self-employed borrowers.

You've done the hard work of building income outside the traditional system. Getting a mortgage is just one more thing to figure out.
